For the last 5 years K-State has been experiencing a decline in its overall enrollment, and despite many efforts from the university to change the decline the skid only continues as 2019 showed another decrease in enrollment.

Fall Enrollment through the Years

According to the K-State Enrollment statistics found on the Office of Registrars web page from the Fall semester of 2018-2019 to 2019-2020, we saw a 2.2% decrease in enrollment. The past few years have been seeing a steady drop in enrollment of around 2% while showing no signs of recovery.

While these statistics are a worrying trend after coronavirus ravaged the earth, colleges all around the United States are seeing drops and, K-State isn't exempt from this. According to the Office of Registrar, K-State enrollment dropped 4% this fall a loss of 865 incoming students.

While many would consider this a worrying sign of the future Provost and Executive Vice President of Kansas State believes that the number could have been even worse.

"I expected a larger enrollment decline following COVID, and the 4% decline was actually a positive sign relative to my expectations."

In February 2019, President Richard Myers spoke with the Rotary Club of Topeka about how decreasing state aid to universities K-State forced an increase of tuition to help fund university operations. Because of the recent increases, many would assume that's the main reason enrollment has been on the decline.

According to the data gathered from the Office of Registrar, many colleges here at K-State took hits to their enrollment in the year 2019. The hardest-hit college was the College of Agriculture which saw a7.7% decrease in its enrollment. The College of Arts and Sciences also saw a large decline in enrollment at 5.8%.

Countermeasures

To counteract the recent downturn in enrollment K-State has been creating many programs to entice new students to the university like reducing the GPA requirements for the 2020-2021 semester.

With Covid-19 being the main proprietor for the most recent drop Taber outlined changes on the horizon to address the issues.

"We are improving communication with students through better technology; we have chosen not to increase tuition for several years in a row, and we have begun a Tuition and Fee Structure Task Force to develop a new tuition and fees approach; we have worked with faculty to provide the best educational experience that we can under current conditions."
